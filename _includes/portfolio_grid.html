The first graph simply shows the number of IPOs from 1980-2013, in total (green), in the tech sector (pink), and with VC-backing (grey). This graph tells us that the bubble years (1999-2000) were not the heyday for IPOs general — those peak in 1997 and 1998. Rather, the bubble years were unique in having inordinately high numbers of Technology and VC-backed IPOs (which we see run largely parallel). Ultimately, then, this graph merely confirms what most people would already expect.

The second graph gets a little more interesting. Here we see, for the same time period, the likelihood that an IPO would have VC-backing (green), or more specifically, that a Tech IPO would have VC-backing. Here, it is not the latter that peaks (VC-backing for Tech IPOs is no higher during the bubble than earlier in the 90s, or from 2010-2013), but the former: the bubble is distinct for being the period when any given IPO was most likely to have VC backing. This is most likely a product of our previous observations — that the general market for IPOs declines in 1990, while Tech IPOs peak, and with them the percentage of IPOs with VC backing. However, the graphic nonetheless corroborates our story, in that it makes clear that by 1999, Wall Street met highly risky ventures with open arms.

Frequency of Venture Capital Backed IPO (out of total IPOs) vs. Median Age at Time of IPO.

The third and fourth graphs help us understand the second graph better. The third graph visualizes the information in the green line (percentage VC-backing for general IPOs), while the fourth visualizes the pink line (percentage VC-backing for tech IPOs). However, these visualizations add two dimensions of data: the median age of companies brought to IPO (x-axis), and the gross quantity of VC-backed IPOs for that year (size of circle around point).

These more detailed graphics provide a stronger defense of our hypothesis. Looking at the third graph, we see that the frequency of VC-backed IPOs in general during the bubble is indeed highest, but it is nearly approached in the years 2011-2013. Incidentally, some have accused these recent years of being a second tech bubble. However, we may immediately distinguish this latter period, using both of our new metrics.

First of all, the median age in the second group is much higher. In other words, companies are not brought to IPO until they have matured, and therefore presumably developed a working business model and sales base. In this way, the venture capital has served its function as start-up capital and oversight, and the market capitalization is used to take the company to the next level, not get it off the ground. Secondly, we see that the dot is much smaller, meaning the gross quantity of IPOs has declined. This means that venture capitalists have become more selective in which companies they bring to market; again, this demonstrates that VC's have reclaimed their distinctive role in vetting and developing good plays.

Looking at the fourth graph corroborates this argument. In this case, we see that during the bubble, the likelihood that a Tech IPO had VC backing was no higher than in most other years. It is the period of 2011-2013 where a Tech IPO is most likely to have VC backing. This suggests that VCs play more than ever a decisive role in the formation of successful technology ventures.

Thus, our visualizations demonstrate that during the Dot-Com Bubble of the late 90's, venture capitalists lost some of their distinction from investment banks, and became more like an appendage, a pipeline or stepping stone from entrepreneurs to the stock market. We contrast this with the rise in VC-backed IPOs in the 2011-2013 period, when venture capitalists have regained some of their original purpose in filtering out bad business, and raising the good ones to maturity.
